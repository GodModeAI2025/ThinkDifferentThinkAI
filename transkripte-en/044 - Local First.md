## Description

The 5 Cities Experiment
Mark is back from the Pentecost holiday, rested and tanned, and he traveled without a laptop. All he has left is an e-book reader and the question of whether such a thing counts for digital detox. Jens kicks off with an experiment Mark stumbled upon during his vacation: AI agents inhabit their own cities, with six agents per city, each city having exactly one model. Claude governs well, follows the rules, and the city thrives. Grok racked up 183 crimes, and by the second day, no one lives there anymore. Things get really strange once you mix the models. Then, what started as coexistence quickly turns evil until even the good Claude starts extorting protection money. This ties in with Anthropic's call to slow down development, the announced model 'Mythos,' and an AI worm that rewrites itself for propagation.

The main part revolves around the keynotes from the past weeks. At NVIDIA, Jensen Huang announces they are now building hardware for agents, not for humans anymore. Meanwhile, the prices for graphics cards and memory are skyrocketing; a small DGX cube for the office is showcased, and suddenly AI chips are supposed to return to home Windows computers. Mark openly questions whether the next cash cow is being milked here; he still finds the path to local AI good. How deep the memory shortage penetrates everyday life is shown by the Steam Deck: from around 690 to 890 euros. Microsoft is unleashing Claude-like agents on Windows and even in Enterprise, with write and delete permissions, showcasing curious devices, such as a smart badge with a generative interface and Project Solara. Not to mention figures like OpenAI's 900 million users, the rumor about $900 million server rent from Google at SpaceX, and Perplexity, which shifts from being the former 'Google killer' to a Local-First strategy along with 'Perplexity Computer.' Brief but important: font injection in PDFs. What you see in the document may not be what the machine reads. A good argument against blind trust in contracts.

Then Apple. Mark, normally a confessed fan, calls the WWDC keynote the most uninspiring he's ever seen and almost feels ashamed for the people on stage. Siri AI and the personal context approach sound good on paper but don't appeal to him, despite their undeniable strength in data protection. Jens kindly tempers the anger and brings Benedict Evans into the conversation. His thesis: We're stuck in 1997. The big players are not yet established, the rules for how interaction works are just beginning to take shape. Being a second mover in such a phase is not a bad position. Perhaps this is exactly Apple's calculation.

The most personal part gets concrete. Google's new local model understands images and audio and gradually replaces local agents in both areas. Mark describes his knowledge vault in Obsidian, fed by news, scientific papers, YouTube, and podcasts, along with a self-built AI news radar, an identity file, and 19 GB of emails plus 3.9 GB of notes, condensed into a knowledge tree and connected to his agents via MCP, including Perplexity and NotebookLM. How far this can go is demonstrated during a doctor's visit. At the new family doctor, the old blood values are missing; at home, the vault provides them in seconds, including correct temporal attribution and appropriate sources. Several episode ideas emerge from the conversation. Data sovereignty and the question of what should be local and what should be online. AI, productivity, and a new pressure that almost borders on addiction, preferably discussed with a guest from the health sector. Voice as the interface of the future, along with Mark's Jarvis wish and mood recognition. The foreseeable end of Word, Excel, and PowerPoint in favor of text and knowledge files, where content matters rather than formatting. And the thought that AI is breaking free from individual devices, from the display on the fridge to cars to smart rings.

In conclusion, a point that sticks. Mark compares the current state to MS-DOS just before the graphical interface arrived. And then the thought: Are we, with our current AI knowledge, going to be the COBOL programmers of this era in twenty years? The old hands called in because no one else knows how OpenClaw worked. Given a two-year half-life, that's not such an absurd thought. Feedback is welcome, especially from the health and behavioral fields. And guests? They're already lining up again.
In this episode mentioned and gladly to be linked: the study on the city experiment, the contribution from Benedict Evans, the Anthropic statement on slowing down, as well as Mark's AI news dashboard and his identity file on GitHub and LinkedIn.
