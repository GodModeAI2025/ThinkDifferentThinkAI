## Description

May I do that?
After episodes with software developers and product people, Mark and Jens this time bring someone to the microphone who looks at AI from a completely different angle: Max, a lawyer specializing in AI and data law. Instead of a caution-caution-caution apostle mode, it's about how law and compliance can play on the field and where the real sticking points are.

Topics of the episode

From blocker to enabler: Why lawyers need to be on the field instead of issuing assessments from an ivory tower. And why a digital mindset is also needed in the legal department.

"It depends": The classic. Hallucinations, liability, and the question of whether an AI output with 70 percent quality might be completely fine in non-critical processes.
Platitudes and fear – Marc's TikTok anecdote about the lawyer with a fountain pen who mainly warns. Why you shouldn't approach people like this and where skill-building needs to start broadly.

"Data protection doesn't allow that": The biggest platitude. Data protection is not a sacred cow, but stands equally beside other legal goods. US hosting is also no longer a showstopper.
Users, operators, providers – Max explains when you slip into which role in the AI Act. Local model with n8n? User. Placing an AI system on the market? Provider with real homework – including user manual, the shape of which no one knows yet.

AI Act in reset: A regulation that isn't really in effect yet and is already being revised. How Brussels compromises work and why often bizarre regulations come out in the end.
Principles instead of individual cases – How Max builds governance in the company: not answering each question individually ("may I do that?"), but setting guardrails and representing them with consistency. Creating security, not uncertainty.

Max in practice: Which AI he uses professionally: everyday AI for research and access to knowledge plus Noxtua, a legal AI system from Germany that has been trained on legal data. Privately: fact-checking friends who "know everything". Live, on the side, at the table.

Household exemption: Sounds like the finance ministry, but is practically: In a purely private context, the GDPR does not apply. But as soon as the dictation device starts, it becomes criminally interesting.
AI glasses, guardian modes, and recordings – What happens when the plot pin runs out in the park and starts up again in the restaurant? Intent, negligence, and the topics we will continue to deal with.

Making knowledge consumable for AI: The most exciting question for Max: Legal advice today is in emails, on the audio track, in the intranet that no one reads. How does that get into a form that others can use with their AI?
How the legal profession is changing – In five years, lawyers won't review contracts or draft clauses anymore. They will bring experience, empathy, and strategic foresight. Or, as Jens puts it sharply: They will become orchestrators of agentic networks.

Mentioned in the episode

AI Act / EU AI Regulation
General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)
Household exemption in data protection law
Noxtua – legal AI system from Germany
n8n
Tesla guardian mode
AI glasses and wearables (Plot Pin)

Take-Aways

Data protection is not a sacred cow – it stands equally next to other legal goods. With good documentation and a thorough assessment, many things are possible.

Most users ask the wrong question: "Am I allowed to do that?" seeks binary security. Better are principles and guardrails.

The AI Act distinguishes between users, operators, and providers – the greatest obligations fall on those who place AI as a product in the market.

US hosting alone is no longer a showstopper. The old platitudes are no longer true.

In purely private contexts, the GDPR does not apply – but be cautious with recordings of other individuals.

In five years, no lawyer will review contracts anymore. What remains is experience, strategy, and orchestration.
