## Description

From Conductor to Composer: Leadership in the Age of Agents (with René Deist)
Who actually leads, if every employee soon has a hundred agents behind him? For the third time, Dr. René Deist sits with Mark and Jens in the virtual studio, this time with a concrete reason: his new book "Who leads here?". He consciously wrote it thin, legible on an intercontinental flight, addressed to the C-level and without a technological deep bore. Mark notes that a sufficiently late railway connection does it too. Jens had the book with him during his holiday on the Kindle, together with, well, a Claude code installation, from which his wife better not know anything.

The central image of the episode comes from René's world of music. Leadership works like a conductor today: the corporate strategy is the score, the talents sit in the orchestra, and the task of the executive is to make something good-sounding out of it, in which everyone can unfold. That's what's happening. René is quoting a Gartner forecast, after which every employee will soon have 109 agents (the crooked number will be blasphemed in the wake of it). If that's true, no one conducts anymore, but every individual in the orchestra has to formulate strategies himself, so compose. Mark raises a counter question: Isn't it rather an orchestra in the orchestra if every flutist can place his own complete ensemble? René's answer: at the core, yes, and the good news is that this transformation does not come overnight.

René's starting point remains the same: AI doesn't want anything. She doesn't have an intention and can't assess any risk in herself because she doesn't feel it. Using the example of purchasing, this means that man formulates what raw material is to be negotiated in which volume at which suppliers. The agents write, evaluate answers, ask gaps and ultimately deliver the large Excel file. And then a person looks at it and notices that the screw forging can't deliver three trillion screws. From this René derives an educational goal: Whoever works in purchasing today develops into a master in intent or master in check and control over the next few years. In between, the work that Mark is particularly interested in, namely who actually builds the agents. René's answer: The experts themselves have to learn to break down their work steps in such a way that skills become part of it, while the IT contributes the framework, including LLM as a Judge and scoring models that test every automation step.

The second thesis of the book is called Prompt Thinking, and René provides an anecdote that probably many people know. Someone asks him how to ensure quality when AI processes drive autonomously, and pushes behind as a justification, he has tried five times to build a PowerPoint, and then given up. René's objection: "Make a PowerPoint with a strategy for bla, produces a randomized result, four pages prompt with role, experience, data source, structure and look and feel produce a controllable one. Mark adds the important limitation from his point of view: Prompt Thinking works especially where one has mastered his craft anyway. If one has not previously built good slides, it does not get them from the AI either. Jens reminds that "Let me Google that for you". Then René makes the paradox out of the research: It needs more executives, no less because suddenly everyone has to lead his hundred agents. If two departments work together, then two people are sitting in the team, but 218 agents. That automation means at the same time, more tasks can not be done with fewer people, as if he does not have an open mind.

The advice to all those who listen and lead: AI Literacy is a leadership issue, no more and no less than when dealing with the calculator at the time. Yes, people have been able to add worse, says René, it was nevertheless correct. This includes for him a leadership understanding as a service rather than a kingship, the hiring of people who are smarter than you yourself, and the space to play around for your own employees. Jens adds that in this environment everyone is learning anyway, which is why it is no shame when the knowledge flows from below. When looking at the risks René contradicts the thesis of the two-class society at least for the Western world. He sees a democratization, keyword one-person trillion-dollar company. The real dangers lie for him elsewhere: to deal with it too late, and the dependence on those who control the technology and thus later make the prices. This is mentioned a petition by about 300 undersigns, including 15 Nobel prize winners as well as people from OpenAI and Google, who call on governments not to stop structural change, but to lead it.

The most compelling part comes to the end. René advises to try out ChatGPT's dialogue mode once, because in his opinion it is hard to distinguish it from a real conversation. It is precisely from this that there is a market in which relationships are sold, even with apps that target children and offer a freely designed companion, from teddy bear to the real person. René's position is unmistakable: this form of AI does not belong in children's hands. Growing-ups need friction, they have to endure that something does not work, and invest in friendships. A companion never quarrels who wants to please. His practical advice comes from his own life: every app that his sons have installed, he has also installed and played, including some sabothy games. Mark spans the bow back to the Toy Wars episode and to the question that the book actually leads to?: Leadership in the age of autonomous AI gives the title: At which places does one actually lead one's own life, and by whom can one be led?
